Sound-makers in
the collective.

Composers, producers, performers and sound designers using AI to expand what music can be — without giving up what makes it human.

AI doesn't
hear the music.
You do.

Every musician in AI201 started from the same place: curiosity about what these tools could add, and scepticism about what they'd take away. The answer, so far, is that they add a lot and take away nothing — as long as you stay in charge.

The composers here use AI to generate harmonic material they react to. The producers use it to find patterns in their own archives and samples. The performers use it to create instruments that respond to them in real time. None of them use it to write music for them.

K
Kemi Adeyemi
Sound Design · Performance

Kemi is a sound designer and performer working at the intersection of audience behaviour and musical composition. Her Crowd Composition project — performed at the Barbican in 2025 — turned the breathing, movement and ambient sound of 300 audience members into live musical notation performed by a quartet. She's currently working on a piece scored entirely by rainfall data from seven London weather stations.

How she uses AI

"I don't use AI to make music. I use it to listen to things I can't hear myself — patterns in data that wouldn't be audible without translation. The music comes from what I decide to do with what it shows me."

P
Priya Nair
Composition · Electronic

Classically trained, experimentally wired. Priya uses AI to generate harmonic material that she then scores and arranges by hand. Her project Oblique Strategies Alive extends Brian Eno's famous creative prompts using language models that understand what you're actually stuck on.

How she uses AI

"It gives me a starting point I wouldn't have chosen myself. Which is the whole point."

D
Dan Kowalski
Production · Beat-making

Dan makes beats. Mostly human ones. He uses AI for stem separation on old records he wants to sample cleanly, and for finding patterns in his own archive of unfinished tracks — the AI finds what connects them, he decides if he agrees.

How he uses AI

"I have 800 unfinished tracks. The AI found three families of ideas I didn't know I was working on. That was useful."

Z
Zara Hussain
Electronic Music · Instrument Design

Zara builds instruments that play themselves and then argues with them about the results. Her modular synth setup includes several AI-driven modules that respond to her playing in real time — not to anticipate or accompany, but to disagree.

How she uses AI

"I want something that pushes back. The best collaborators do. The AI is the bandmate that never agrees with my first idea."

M
Mo Yusuf
Jazz · Generative Harmony

Mo is a jazz pianist who uses AI to generate harmonic substitutions he'd never have considered — not to play them, but to hear what they suggest. The ideas are machine-generated. The music is entirely his.

How he uses AI

"It's like having a theory textbook that answers back. Useful for getting out of your own head."

T
Thandi Okoye
Afrobeats · Production

Thandi produces Afrobeats and uses AI primarily to explore what happens when you cross-pollinate rhythmic traditions — West African percussion patterns with electronic production frameworks — at a speed that would be impossible by hand.

How she uses AI

"It helps me explore faster. The choices are always mine. The speed is the tool."
